WHAT'S TRENDING

In all generations, there have been daring scientists who have pushed boundaries and achieved great breakthroughs. While many of these scientists have been called geniuses in history, others have been called mad. Whether in fiction or in real life, scientists have endeavored towards achieving new discoveries. It is different matter that not all of these discoveries have worked or seem logical. When it comes to real-life scientists, eccentricity and brilliance seem to go hand in hand. History has witnessed some of the greatest scientists who can be considered as mad. One of the real-life scientists who earned the reputation of a mad scientist during his time was the Serbian-American inventor Nikola Tesla. He is majorly known for his contributions in inventing the AC generator and the wireless radio. Tesla’s name often conjures an image of a man pulling down a giant electric switch in a shower of fiery sparks. Tesla also loved putting on a good show for his audience, often using his own body as a conductor during public demonstrations. Richard Feynman is another person who earned the reputation of a mad scientist. He was one of the scientists in the US government’s Manhattan Project that was responsible for developing the atomic bomb. Feynman was a free-spirited man, who explored music and nature, decoded Mayan hieroglyphics, and picked locks in his spare time. Another mad scientist in history was Sidney Gottlieb. He was an American chemist with knowledge in knowledge, and headed the US Central Intelligence Agency’s technical services division. Gottlieb was a master of poisons and believed that influencing and controlling the human mind could help the US in attaining global dominance. Gottlieb hatched schemes of assassinating Cuban revolutionary leader Fidel Castro by poisoning his cigar. Another person who can be considered as a mad scientist is Dr. Robert J. White. He was an American neurosurgeon best known as the first to successfully remove the brain of a living dog and keeping it alive outside the body. A few years later, White transplanted a canine brain into another dog’s neck, thus surgically providing the dog with two brains.


Scientists who are ahead of their time are often neglected, scorned, or even ridiculed by society. However, many of these daring inventors have made such remarkable scientific breakthroughs that have profoundly shaped the future of mankind and significantly transformed the modern world. When one looks at innovations, such as the electric light bulb, airplanes, telephone, television, and more recent personal computers, which were savagely mocked when they first came out, one is reminded of the importance of supporting the crazy inventors. Breakthrough inventions take time, effort, and patience to come to fruition. Inventors generally have excessive confidence in their ideas, but many a times these ideas do not translate into effective designs in one go. A sustained financial support, possibly unconditional and benevolent from public-spirited funders, can help innovators in improvising on their prototype and adding new functionalities and features to it. In addition, innovations can be encouraged by providing tax relief to inventors for setting up research and development labs. Moreover, stories of successful innovators should be included in textbooks for inspiring and igniting the mind of young aspiring inventors. It is very possible that several of the ideas presented by inventors may appear to be irrational, unviable, whimsical, or even socially undesirable. Equal space should be provided for inventors to nurture rational as well as irrational ideas for fueling the human spirit to achieve the unthinkable.

  • Refrigeration - The first refrigeration machine was developed in the year 1755 by a Scotland-based professor, William Cullen. The refrigeration was designed with a pump for creating an incomplete vacuum over a diethyl ether container, which absorbs heat from the nearby air on boiling. However, the first refrigeration machine that made use of vapor and not liquid for cooling was developed in the year 1805 by Cullen. The first refrigeration machine that was developed for practical refrigeration was designed in 1834 by Jacob Parkins, and he was the first person to hold a patent for his invention.
  • Toothbrush - Prior to the invention of toothbrushes, people made use of chew sticks, and twigs with tattered ends for brushing their teeth. The chew sticks were used for rubbing against and cleaning the teeth. Bristle toothbrushes very much similar to the present ones were invented in China in the year 1498. Bristles back then were very rigid, and uneven hairs were taken from behind the hog’s neck and hooked onto the handles that were made of bamboo or bone. Until 1938, boar bristles were only used and the nylon bristles were launched in the same year by Dupont de Nemours. The first nylon toothbrush that was launched was named Doctor West`s Miracle Toothbrush. Subsequently, impressed by the oral hygiene habits of the soldiers from world war II, individuals increasingly adopted nylon toothbrushes for maintaining good oral hygiene.
  • Three-Position Traffic Lights - The first-ever traffic signal was invented in London in the year 1868, however, the contemporary traffic signal was launched by a policeman from Detroit, William Potts. This traffic light was developed using a few switches and wires, and 3 colors of railroad lights and was popularly used for controlling traffic at Michigan and at the corner of Woodward. However, the system was patented by Garret Morgan in 1923, which was soon bought by General Electric (GE), and since then the company was developing as well as marketing traffic signals to the national market.
  • Air Conditioner - Air conditioner was invented by Willis Carrier in the year 1902. He was the first person to invent a method for controlling humidity, which is the basic building block for contemporary air conditioners. Carrier’s invention gave birth to several industries that rule the economy today. Production of several things ranging from baked goods to wartime essentials was also produced thanks to the air conditioning. The accurate control of humidity and temperature provided by the air conditioners made it very popular for use in cinema theaters, transatlantic flights, servers, computer rooms, and shopping malls also.
  • Syringe - Syringes were initially used by Romans, however, the first contemporary version was launched in 1650, when Blaise Pascal was experimenting with hydrodynamics. The device developed by Pascal demonstrated the law of physics which came to be known as Pascal’s laws. As per the law whenever there is a rise in pressure at a given point in a limited liquid, there is an equal rise at every other point in the vessel. The first hollow steel needle was invented by Francis Rynd in 1844 for injecting medicine, while the first hypodermic needle was invented in 1853.
  • Zipper - Elias Howe was the person behind who invented of the sewing machine and in 1851, he received a patent for "Automatic, Continuous Clothing Closure". Moving forward, Whitcomb Judson marketed the "Clasp Locker" device in 1893 that was similar to Howe’s model but he was the first one to market as is hence called the "inventor of the zipper," however, the term zipper was used majorly until B. F. Goodrich Company started using this term. Gideon Sundback launched a novel and improved system Separable Fastener in 1917.

Baldwin utilized the idea for a telephonic feature for developing an even more subtle telephonic receiver in 1910 and subsequently retailed the versions to the US Navy on a contract basis. The headphones sent by Baldwin were found to be highly useful and practicable in comparison to the model that was used by Naval radio operators at that time. Later on, Wireless Specialty Apparatus Co. partnered with Baldwin for manufacturing headphones. The first marketable and viable stereo headphones were launched in 1957 by EMI.

However, during the 20th-century individuals deciphered that hot water and cold water both could be blended into a single fixture. Alfred Moen in the year 1937 invented the first single-handle faucet that was capable of blending hot as well as cold water prior to leaving the fixture or pipe.
  • Mason Jars - John L. Mason invented the mason jars that are used even today in the year 1858. The machine was invented that was capable of cutting threads into lids, which made it possible for manufacturing a jar with recyclable and screw-on lids. The sealing mechanism was the biggest difference between the jars invented by Mason and the previous jars. A glass container was developed with a thread that was molded into its top part and a lid made of zinc was designed with a rubber ring. The seal created by the rubber was maintained by the threaded lid. These jars were popularly used for home storage of various things such as tomatoes, pickles, and relishes.

  • Vaccinations - Edward Jenner made use of cowpox material for developing immunity to smallpox. The method developed by him underwent major technological and medical alterations and led to the elimination of smallpox eventually. This was considered the first vaccine in the world. The rabies vaccine developed by Louis Pasteur in 1885 was the next vaccine that made a great impact on human diseases. Subsequently, vaccines and antitoxins against tuberculosis, typhoid, plague, cholera, anthrax, tetanus, and diphtheria were developed through the 1930s.
