WHAT'S TRENDING

The startup ecosystem in Europe is extremely diverse and complex. Europe is home to several thriving startup ecosystems, whether at national level or concentrated in specific areas, with each ecosystem having its own focus and uniqueness. Countries, such as Sweden, Spain, the Netherlands, Switzerland, Germany, and the UK, have consistently stood out as the top European as well as global startup ecosystems. The UK’s startup ecosystem has been continuously growing and is currently considered to be among the top-three in the world, attributed to the exceptional place that the capital city London holds for startup development. London’s startup ecosystem has demonstrated its resilience despite Brexit. The city is also the hub of the European VC industry, as well as has developed a mature system for supporting young entrepreneurs through government funding. The UK’s withdrawal from the EU paved way for Germany to emerge as the next major startup ecosystem and entrepreneurial hub. In comparison to most of the other large cities in Europe, German cities continue to offer cost-effective living options as well as have been able to attract a robust pool of global talent. Growing interest in entrepreneurship and startup innovation, favorable government support, and the country’s strong relationship to several global investment groups have led to the emergence of several German cities in the top global startup ecosystems.


Some of the best European cities for startup development include London, Moscow, Berlin, Paris, Amsterdam, Barcelona, and Stockholm. London’s startup ecosystem encompasses innovators across various sectors, including banking, fintech, ecommerce, and software, boasts strong connections with the North American markets. The city ranks as the world’s third-leading tech hub, the second-leading fintech hub, and the fourth-leading edtech hub. In 2021, London emerged as Europe’s unicorn capital when the total unicorn count in the city scaled to 75. There are various initiatives that the city of London offers to support tech startups, including the Resilience Fund, a £1 million challenge fund aimed to incentivize innovators to address key challenging faced by the city, and the Business Growth Program, which is focused on providing financial support to early-stage startups with less than 10 employees. London ecosystem provides startups an easy access to affluent consumers and powerful corporations. In addition, the city offers a large population of highly-skilled workforce, both domestic talent due to the presence of several prestigious universities and the international talent that the city attracts owing to its strong global reputation. In 2021, London attracted tech investment worth more than €11 billion, substantially surpassing the combined investment in Paris and Berlin. However, the high cost of living and salaries, along with the lack of affordable coworking spaces and incubators, are some of the major challenges that startups generally face while operating in London. Some of the notable London-based startups include Lottie, HumanForest, and Zilch. Lottie offers a free-of-cost digital service to help retirees and families find the best elderly care homes and retirement living communities in the country. The company, founded in 2021, has been continuously securing funding that has led its valuation to reach over €52 million. HumanForest is engaged in delivering a shared electric bike service in the city of London. In early-2022, the company announced entering into partnership with food delivery unicorn Deliveroo for boosting affordable and sustainable delivery services across London. Founded in 2019, the company had raised €1.4 million via a Crowdcube campaign in late-2020. Zilch is one of the fastest-growing fintechs in Europe offering a unique next-generation payment and buy now, pay later (BNPL) platform that works directly with consumers. Founded in 2018, Zilch, a double unicorn, secured an additional US$50 million in funding in mid-2022, taking its total Series C funding to US$153 million.


In recent years, Berlin has emerged as a major hub for startups both in Germany as well as Europe, with nearly 22% of all startups in the country being established in the city. Berlin’s startup ecosystem benefits from the city’s diversity and presence of several universities. Berlin actively offers support to startups both in the pre-startup and early-stage period. For instance, Berlin Startup Scholarship, a program financed by the state of Berlin with the support of the European Social Fund, offers financial support to tech-based startups. In addition, the investment division of Investitionsbank Berlin has been investing in innovative startups. Some of the notable Berlin-based startups include Ecoligo GmbH, Grover GmbH, and Zenjob GmbH. Ecoligo is an impact investment provider that implements solar-as-a-service projects in emerging markets. The company, founded in 2016, recently secured funding of €10 million from investors. Grover, on the other hand, is involved in providing an online platform for renting tech products through monthly subscriptions. The company, founded in 2015, recently received €270 million in funding. Zenjob, founded in 2015, offers an online staffing platform to help students and workers to find flexible part-time jobs. The company recently raised €45 million in a growth funding round that would be utilized to expand its presence across Europe.


Capital cities have been at the spotlight of startup development in Europe and France is no exception. Paris ranks among the globally leading startup ecosystems, driven by governmental support through tax incentives and other initiatives, along with the growing popularity of the entrepreneurship mindset. The city is home to close to 12,000 startups and the number is continuously growing with the French government allocating large sums of money in its budget for supporting startup development. In addition, the government reduced the corporate tax to 25% in 2022, thus making France an attractive destination for both domestic as well as international startups. Other initiatives, such as a 30% tax credit to tech startups on their initial investment are also encouraging the growth of the French startup ecosystem. Paris also benefits from the presence of a renowned engineering university and several top-class business schools, all of which are contributing in fostering domestic talent as well as innovation. Some of the prominent Paris-based startups include Upway, Back Market, and Gourmey. Upway, which offers an online marketplace for second-hand electric bikes, raised funding valued at €23 million in mid-2022 to be utilized for expanding the company’s presence in the US, the Netherlands, and Germany by end-2022. Back Market, founded in 2014, offers an online marketplace for refurbished electronic appliances and devices. In early-2022, the company raised €449 million and established itself as one of the most valuable startups in France, with a valuation of more than €5 billion. Gourmey, on the other hand, is engaged in developing lab-grown, non-animal meat. The company, launched in 2019, secured €48 million in Series A funding in late-2022. Amsterdam is another European city with a vibrant startup ecosystem, comprising several young entrepreneurs and a large number of female founders. Amsterdam startup ecosystem covers various sectors, ranging from traditional industries to new sectors, such as green-tech. Moscow also ranks among the top European and global startup ecosystems, primarily attributed to significant government support, in conjunction with the city’s focus on retention and scaling via access to a massive domestic market. However, Russia’s geopolitical situation continues to pose challenges for the startup ecosystem in Moscow, which many startup facing travel and financial restrictions while planning international expansion.

The European Innovation Council (EIC) recently announced plans to disburse €1.6 billion to innovators and scientists in 2023 with the aim of scaling up breakthrough technologies as well as creating new markets. Around 70% of the total amount, or €1.13 billion, has been earmarked for the EIC Accelerator for supporting SMEs and startups towards the development and marketing of high-impact innovations. Specifically, €525 million has been reserved for startups to develop future technologies that would contribute to the various strategic objectives of the EU, including the New European Bauhaus initiative, space tech, resilient agriculture, semiconductor or quantum components, energy storage, decontamination for pandemic management, and cancer biomarkers. The balance €613 million would be made available for innovations in other areas of technology. The work program of the EIC for 2023 also focuses on other significant objectives, such as increasing the flow of innovative talent into deep tech startups, providing enhanced support to women innovators, and facilitating the collaboration between public and private procurers. The support provided by the EIC to date has resulted in a portfolio of EIC companies achieving a cumulative valuation of more than €40 billion, including 112 centaurs and 12 unicorns. In addition, the EIC funding has contributed in incentivizing more than €10 billion in follow-on investments in its portfolio companies from the private sector. While at the outset the EIC’s funding of €1.13 billion for tech startups might seem a significant amount, several experts are of the opinion that it is not enough given the size of the massive European tech startup ecosystem. It must be noted that private investment in UK deep tech startups alone touched €8.5 billion in 2021, which allowed for the exponential growth in the sector. Experts also note that in case the EIC’s funding of €1.13 billion is allocated equally among the bloc’s 27 members, each country would end up receiving only around €42 million. Industry experts highlight that Europe is home to a large number of tech companies, has a skilled workforce, and equipped with the required infrastructure for establishing itself as a tech powerhouse. However, what is limiting the region in this regard is the lack of adequate financial support. Experts point out the both the local governments as well as the EU would need to further increase funding in the emerging tech startups in case the continent intends on competing with the major players in the US and China.

The European startup ecosystem has been exhibiting a steady growth over the past few years, with several European countries ranking among the world’s leading startup ecosystems. However, the startup scene in Europe is still not at the same level as in other regions across the globe. The leading European hubs considerably lag behind those in the US in terms of talent, capital, concentration, and size. One of the key strengths of the European startup ecosystem is that it is well established offers high attractiveness to a wide range of investors. However, European startups face the challenge of retaining their talent pool, since the continent’s cultural mindset values successful exits, particularly in countries such as France. Close working relationships and ties between various participants in the European ecosystem can play a critical role in fostering growth and innovation, particularly with government relationships facilitating further connections. The relative strength of the European startups in the B2B space and the region increasingly embracing a digital economy would bode well for the European startup ecosystem. Another major strength for the European startup ecosystem is the region’s stronghold in attracting tech funding. Investment in European tech startups increased by a staggering 500% during the period 2012-2017 and touched US$25 billion. London is one of the leading European hubs for fintech startups, and holds tremendous potential in driving technological developments over the next few years. In addition, a large number of European countries have begun offering e-visas and flexible residency permits for attracting entrepreneurs from across the globe. This move is in conjunction with governments offering flexible regulations for supporting the emerging startups. Moreover, Europe’s English proficiency has been consistently strong, with the EU as well as the Schengen Area countries displaying higher levels of English fluency as compared to other countries. High English proficiency can help startup ecosystems in attracting global talent as well as international investors. Despite the positive outlook of the European startup ecosystem, it also has certain limitations. One of the biggest drawbacks of the European startup ecosystem is that it has failed in creating startup giants, such as Microsoft, Facebook, Amazon, and Google. No startup has come even close to the authority and scale of the startups emerging from the US. In addition, the startup ecosystem in Europe has evolved at a slower pace in comparison to the US. While the VC firms in the US were first established in the 1940s, they came into being in Europe only in the mid-1990s. Another major limitation for the European startup ecosystem is the lack of government support, with governments in countries, especially Italy, Greece, and France, imposing excessive and stringent regulations pertaining to taxation and employment, making it difficult for startups to attract and retain talent. Moreover, the culture mindset towards entrepreneurship is less prominent in European countries as compared to the US.

Over the past few years, the European startup ecosystem has witnessed a significant spike not only in terms of the number of unicorns being created, but also the pace at which they are emerging. There were 99 VC-backed unicorns in Europe in 2020, of which 14 were minted in 2019 alone. Some of the prominent startups that earned unicorn status in 2019 include Vinted, a Lithuania-based online marketplace for buying, selling and exchanging used clothing and accessories; Doctolib, a French cloud-based appointment scheduling and patient referral tool; and German neobank N26. However, despite growing at a brisk pace, European startups continue to fall short in accomplishing successful late-stage results in comparison to other regional startup ecosystems. Though Europe’s startup ecosystem is changing quickly, the region is still marred by lesser number of startups, startups raising less money, and lower chances of startups becoming successful in securing Series C funding, going public, or getting acquired. Even though Europe generates 36% of all formally-funded startups, its contribution in the global list of unicorns is only 14%. Adjusted to GDP and population, the number of seed-stage startups generated by Europe is merely 40% of that the US generates. Moreover, European startups have historically been 30% less likely in achieving late-stage successes as compared to their US counterparts. Further, European startups have consistently displayed lower success at progressing through different funding rounds as compared to the startups in countries, such as the US and India. Though startups in Europe are less successful at progressing through funding rounds, they cannot be considered as outright failures. Despite making slow progress in advancing to subsequent funding rounds, European startups are not likely to go out of business more frequently than US startups. European startups are 10% more likely to stall after bagging Series A funding as compared to US startups. This could be because these startups eventually become profitable and start exhibiting growth and hence opt for self-funding. However, this could result in them sacrificing future growth potential. The move could also have a negative effect on venture capitalists, for whom high profitability might not be enough, but achieving late-stage successes through IPOs or large acquisitions might be important for gaining high returns on investment.
